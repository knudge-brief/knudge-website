Every news product I've ever used has answered the same question: *what happened today?*

Some do it with more depth. Some do it faster. Some filter by topic, or region, or format. But the output is always the same — a better-organised version of what happened.

That's not nothing. But it's not enough.

---

Here's the question I kept asking myself after reading the news: *so what?*

Interest rates went up. So what — for me, specifically, right now? A startup I'd never heard of raised $200M. Should I care? A policy changed in a sector I work adjacent to. Does that affect me, or not?

The news never answers that question. It can't. It's written for everyone.

**Written for everyone means written for no one in particular.**

---

## The format is the problem, not the content

We've spent years trying to fix news by improving the content layer. Better sourcing. Fact-checking. Faster delivery. Multilingual coverage.

All of that matters. But the fundamental model hasn't changed: publish information, let the reader figure out relevance.

The reader is supposed to do the work of translating *event* → *impact on my life*. That translation is hard. Most of us don't do it. We skim, we absorb vaguely, we move on.

And we walk away feeling informed without actually being equipped to act.

---

## What knudge is an attempt to fix

The insight behind knudge is simple: the translation layer should be built into the product, not offloaded to the reader.

If I know who you are — your job, your city, your industry, your financial situation, your goals — then "interest rates went up" becomes a very specific sentence with a very specific implication for you. Or it becomes irrelevant. Either way, I can tell you which.

That's what we're building toward. Not a smarter news aggregator. Not a better newsletter. A product that knows who you are and translates the world accordingly.

---

## Why we're starting small

We haven't built the app yet. We're not going to, not immediately.

First, we want to prove the concept works with real people. That means sending briefs — manually, semi-automated — to a small group of people whose profiles we know. Watching whether it actually changes how they think about what they read. Seeing if personalisation at this level is as valuable as we think it is.

If it is, we build. If it's not, we learn something important before writing a single line of code.

That's the approach. Come watch — or better, come participate.
